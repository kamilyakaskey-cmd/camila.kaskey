Automated Hamburger Flipper: Detection, Probing & Flipping System

An engineering project built for Theta Tau's pledge process — a gantry-style machine that mounts over a grill, locates a patty using a laser sensor array, probes its internal temperature, and flips it automatically onto a plate.

01 · Project Overview

Overview

The Automated Hamburger Flipper was a team project completed during Spring 2025 as part of Theta Tau's pledge process. As Pledge Class President, I coordinated my team's progress, organized weekly work, contributed to design discussions, and helped fabricate and assemble the prototype alongside my teammates.

Our goal was to build an automated system that could locate a hamburger patty on a grill, monitor its internal temperature, flip it during cooking, and transfer it onto a plate once it was fully cooked. The final design combined mechanical components, sensors, Arduino-based control, and automated motion in a gantry-mounted system.

02 · Concept & Detection Strategy

Design Concept

One of the main design challenges was locating a patty anywhere on the grill without using cameras or manual positioning. Our team designed a gantry-style system mounted above the grill that allowed both the temperature probe and spatula to move independently across the cooking surface.

A laser sensor array determined the patty's X-position. Each laser corresponded to a fixed location on the grill. When a beam was interrupted, the carriage moved to the matching position.

Once aligned, the temperature probe advanced along the Y-axis until it detected a sudden increase in temperature, indicating that it had reached the patty rather than the grill surface. The probe remained in place until the target temperature was reached, then retracted so the spatula could perform the flipping sequence.

Using laser sensors instead of a camera simplified the design while still providing reliable positioning.

03 · CAD Development

CAD Development

Before fabrication, our team modeled the complete system in SolidWorks to verify the layout and make sure every moving component had enough clearance to operate across the grill.

The assembly included the gantry frame, rail system, carriage mechanisms, threaded rod drives, rotating spatula, and temperature probe assembly. Building the full CAD model helped identify potential interference before manufacturing began.

Components such as the rails and spatula were modeled individually before being integrated into the complete assembly.

04 · Mechanical Build

Fabrication & Assembly

After the design was finalized, our team built the prototype using both custom-made and off-the-shelf components. The system was mounted above the grill on a rigid frame that supported the rail assemblies and moving carriages.

I worked in the machine shop cutting, sanding, and preparing parts before assembly. Being involved in the fabrication process gave me hands-on experience with manufacturing, part fitment, and mechanical assembly.

During assembly, several adjustments were made to improve component alignment and reduce resistance in the moving mechanisms, allowing the probe and spatula to travel more smoothly across the rail system.

05 · Sensing & Control

Sensing & Temperature Monitoring

The system uses two sensing methods throughout the cooking process. A laser sensor array determines the patty's X-position by detecting which beam has been interrupted, allowing the carriage to move to the correct location. A DS18B20 waterproof digital temperature probe then monitors the patty's internal temperature. The sensor operates over a range of -55°C to 125°C and interfaces directly with the Arduino controller. Once inserted into the patty, it continuously measures the internal temperature until it reaches 160°F, at which point the control system retracts the probe to clear the path for the rotating spatula before the flipping sequence begins.

06 · Control Logic

How It Operates

The full sequence, programmed in Arduino, runs as follows:

  1. The laser sensor array detects the patty's position along the X-axis by identifying which beam has been interrupted.
  2. The retracted temperature probe moves to the corresponding X-position while remaining clear of the grill.
  3. The spatula travels along its own rail to the same location.
  4. The probe advances across the grill along the Y-axis until it detects a sharp increase in temperature, indicating it has entered the patty.
  5. The probe remains in place while continuously monitoring the internal temperature until the patty reaches 160°F.
  6. The probe retracts to its home position, clearing the path for the spatula.
  7. The spatula rotates and flips the patty.
  8. The probe reinserts into the patty to monitor the second side.
  9. Once the second side reaches the target temperature, the spatula performs a final rotation, lifting the patty from the grill and placing it onto a plate.
07 · Testing & Results

Testing & Results

Each subsystem was tested individually before the full system was assembled. This included checking carriage movement, verifying the laser detection concept, testing probe positioning, and confirming the spatula's range of motion.

Testing also showed that some mechanical adjustments were needed to improve alignment and reduce friction in the moving components. After making these changes, the system operated more consistently and demonstrated that the overall concept could successfully combine sensing, motion, and temperature monitoring.

08 · Results & Lessons Learned

Results & Lessons Learned

This project gave me experience working on a system that combined mechanical design, sensors, electronics, and automation. It also showed me how closely different subsystems depend on one another for the entire machine to work properly.

As Pledge Class President, I learned how to organize a team, keep projects moving, and communicate progress throughout development. I also gained hands-on experience in the machine shop by fabricating parts and helping assemble the prototype.

Building the project from CAD models to a working prototype showed me how design, manufacturing, and assembly influence one another, and how small changes during fabrication can affect the performance of the final system.
